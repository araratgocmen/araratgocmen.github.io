Working Papers



Cross-Pressured Business: Ethnic Attachments of the Family Firm

Why do businesses financially support certain political parties? Extensive research finds that while individuals tend to donate to single political parties expressively or ideologically, companies will tend to donate more strategically, hedging bets across parties, and treating campaign contributions as short term investments for influence and access. I assemble the universe of disclosed corporate donations to political parties in India between 2003-2020, and find that corporate donations can reveal a more expressive logic: over 90% of companies donating to a single political party--puzzling in a highly federalized, competitive multi-party democracy. I explain this non-strategic behavior by recognizing the predominance of the family business in India's corporate landscape. I draw on the finance and economic sociology literatures to develop a political theory of family firms that argues family firms are more than economic entities, but social entities as well. Families are embedded in social networks and relationships, and by concentrated control over a firm's operations in the hands of a single family, the firm's political actions begin to mirror the preferences of the individuals in charge. I employ administrative data on corporate board membership to develop a measure of family involvement on a corporate board, and find family firms are less likely to hedge their bets, preferring to stick to their chosen party, producing donation patterns more similar to individual giving. Next, I study if social embeddings help predict which party family firms support. I develop a measure of firm-party ethnic proximity by using name matching as a proxy for subcaste-matching, and I show how the choice of which political party a firm chooses to donate to is predicted by ethnic proximity. This effect is particularly salient for family firms. These results show how the most ubiquitous type of firm in the world can subordinate economic interests for social identity, raising important challenges for channels of accountability between business and government.
